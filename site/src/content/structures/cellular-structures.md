## Overview

Cellular structures achieve high stiffness-to-weight ratios by distributing material through a
periodic or stochastic network of struts, walls, or plates. The design space spans natural foams
(bone, cork, wood) through engineered lattices (Kelvin cell, octet truss) to topology-optimized
freeform networks.

## Key concepts

- **Relative density** — ratio of cellular material density to that of the solid parent material
- **Gibson–Ashby scaling laws** — mechanical properties scale as powers of relative density
- **Anisotropy** — most cellular solids are stiffer in one direction; exploiting this is a design lever

## Connection to gridshells and space trusses

Space trusses and gridshells can be understood as the limit case of a cellular solid with very low
relative density — essentially a single layer of struts arranged to carry load as a surface structure
rather than a volume.
