## What is a shell structure?

A shell derives its load-carrying capacity from its geometry rather than its cross-section depth.
By curving a thin surface into three dimensions, compressive forces are redirected along the surface
rather than through bending. This is why an eggshell can support many times its own weight while
remaining extraordinarily thin.

## The family tree

```
Shell structures
├── Thin shells (continuous surface — concrete, steel, timber, CLT)
│   ├── Synclastic (dome-like, positive Gaussian curvature)
│   ├── Anticlastic (saddle, negative Gaussian curvature)
│   └── Cylindrical / developable
└── Gridshells (discrete lattice approximating a shell surface)
    ├── Rigid-jointed (steel nodes, glass roofs)
    └── Bending-active (flexible members, erected from flat)
```

## Why shells at domestic scale?

Most shell literature targets large-span civic structures: airports, museums, sports halls.
The structural logic applies equally to sheds, greenhouses, and small homes — and the material
savings are proportionally more meaningful at domestic scale where budgets are tighter.
